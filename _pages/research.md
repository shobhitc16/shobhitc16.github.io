My research program applies computational chemistry and machine learning to understand and engineer enzyme catalysis. Below are the seven interconnected themes that define my work.

---

## 1. Machine Learning & Data-Driven Approaches

I develop machine learning frameworks that leverage three-dimensional electric field data to predict and classify enzymatic function. By training models on electric field fingerprints computed across diverse enzyme families, we demonstrated that electrostatic signatures alone can distinguish between monooxygenases, peroxidases, and catalases — revealing the physical basis of functional divergence in structurally similar enzymes.

**Key publications:**
- *J. Am. Chem. Soc.* 2024, 146, 28375–28383 (ML prediction of enzyme function from electric fields)

---

## 2. Dynamic 3D Electric Field Mapping

Electric fields within enzyme active sites are not static — they fluctuate with protein dynamics and substrate binding. I pioneered computational protocols to map time-resolved, three-dimensional electric field landscapes along reaction coordinates, providing a dynamic picture of how enzymes modulate electrostatic environments to facilitate catalysis.

**Key publications:**
- *J. Am. Chem. Soc.* 2024, 146, 16670–16680 (Electric field evolution in protoglobin directed evolution)
- *Chem. Rev.* 2025 (Comprehensive review of electric fields in enzymes)

---

## 3. Enzyme Design Through Electric Fields & Second Coordination Spheres

A central theme of my work is understanding how second coordination sphere (SCS) residues — amino acids not directly bonded to the catalytic center but positioned nearby — fine-tune enzyme reactivity through electrostatic interactions. By systematically mapping how SCS mutations alter the electric field landscape, I provide a rational framework for enzyme engineering that goes beyond traditional active-site-focused approaches.

**Key publications:**
- *Chemical Science* 2023, 14, 8027–8046 (Review: SCS in metalloenzymes)
- *ACS Central Science* 2020, 6, 2160–2167 (SCS effects in lipoxygenase)

---

## 4. Drug Discovery & Free Energy Simulations

In collaboration with experimental groups, I apply advanced free energy perturbation (FEP) simulations and large-scale DFT calculations to investigate small-molecule binding to disease-relevant protein targets. A current focus involves characterizing the binding of inhibitors targeting &alpha;-synuclein aggregation — a key process in Parkinson's disease — to elucidate mechanisms of action and guide rational drug design. This work extends my electrostatics expertise toward therapeutic applications, bridging fundamental enzyme chemistry with translational drug discovery.

---

## 5. Hybrid QM/MM Reaction Mechanisms

I employ multi-level quantum mechanics/molecular mechanics (QM/MM) simulations to elucidate detailed reaction mechanisms of metalloenzymes. By treating the catalytic core with high-level quantum methods (DFT, DLPNO-CCSD(T)) while embedding it in a classically treated protein environment, these studies reveal how protein structure controls reaction energetics and selectivity.

**Key publications:**
- *JACS Au* 2022, 2, 2326–2340 (QM/MM mechanism of isopenicillin N synthase)
- *Chem. Eur. J.* 2023, 29, e202203713 (Bifurcation in IPNS mechanism)

---

## 6. Classical Molecular Dynamics & Protein Motion Analysis

I use extensive classical molecular dynamics simulations (typically 500 ns–1 &mu;s) to characterize the conformational dynamics of enzymes, including correlated motions, flexibility profiles, and allosteric communication pathways. These simulations provide the statistical ensembles needed for electric field analysis and reveal how protein motions contribute to catalysis.

**Key publications:**
- *ChemPhysChem* 2021, 22, 1073–1081 (MD analysis of lipoxygenase dynamics)
- *J. Phys. Chem. B* 2020, 124, 8149–8159 (Protein dynamics in metalloenzymes)

---

All of my research relies on large-scale high-performance computing resources. I have obtained over $232,000 in ACCESS (formerly XSEDE) computational grants as PI/co-PI, supporting calculations on national supercomputing facilities including SDSC Expanse, NCSA Delta, and PSC Bridges-2. I maintain proficiency in managing complex computational workflows across HPC architectures.

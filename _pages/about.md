I am a computational chemist investigating how **protein scaffolds** — particularly **local electric fields** and **protein dynamics** — shape catalysis and guide evolution. My vision is to integrate **data-driven approaches** (e.g., machine learning) with **multiscale simulations** (QM/MM, MD) on high-performance computing platforms to both elucidate **fundamental mechanisms** of biocatalysis and **engineer enzymes** with novel or enhanced functions.

By capturing and quantifying how the protein's scaffold facilitates catalysis, I seek to enable **rational enzyme design** — whether by reengineering proteins for new-to-nature catalysis or achieving de novo designs for biocatalysis, biotechnology, and therapeutic applications. I also apply **free energy perturbation** and **large-scale DFT** calculations to drug discovery problems, including small-molecule inhibitors targeting protein aggregation.

Currently a Postdoctoral Scholar in the [Alexandrova Group](https://www.chemistry.ucla.edu/directory/alexandrova-anastassia-n) at UCLA, I develop computational frameworks that map three-dimensional electric field landscapes within enzyme active sites and use machine learning to predict enzymatic function from electrostatic signatures alone. I received my Ph.D. in Chemistry from [Michigan Technological University](https://www.mtu.edu/chemistry/) (2022), where I worked with [Prof. Christo Z. Christov](https://www.mtu.edu/chemistry/department/faculty/christov/) on QM/MM studies of metalloenzyme mechanisms.
